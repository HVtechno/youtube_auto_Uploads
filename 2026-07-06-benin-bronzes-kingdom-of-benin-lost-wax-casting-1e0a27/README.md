# Benin’s Bronze Memory Keepers

In the Kingdom of Benin, master casters turned metal into history using the lost-wax method. Their plaques and figures recorded kings, rituals, trade, and power long before photography or film. #BeninBronzes #AncientCraft #AfricanHistory #ForgottenWisdom #ArtHistory

## Scenes

**Scene 1**
- Narration: What if a kingdom kept its history not in books, but in metal? In the Kingdom of Benin, artists cast bronze plaques that remembered rulers, ceremonies, soldiers, and visitors.
- Image prompt: Close-up of an Edo bronze caster holding a finished rectangular bronze plaque showing a Benin court scene, inside a traditional workshop in the Kingdom of Benin, warm morning light, historically appropriate Edo clothing, no on-image text

**Scene 2**
- Narration: From about the 13th century, Benin grew into a powerful Edo kingdom in what is now southern Nigeria. Its royal court supported specialized guilds, including the Igun Eromwon, the bronze casters.
- Image prompt: Wide shot of the royal court of the Kingdom of Benin with palace courtyards, coral-adorned nobles, and bronze casters gathered near a workshop, late afternoon light, historically appropriate Edo architecture and attire

**Scene 3**
- Narration: Their method was lost-wax casting. A sculptor shaped the image in beeswax, covered it in clay, heated it so the wax melted out, then poured molten metal into the hollow form.
- Image prompt: Medium shot of a Benin bronze caster pouring molten metal from a small crucible into a clay mold beside a charcoal furnace, dim orange firelight, traditional Edo workshop, historically appropriate tools and clothing

**Scene 4**
- Narration: This was remarkable because each cast object was both art and archive. Plaques helped the palace remember lineages, victories, rituals, trade contacts, and the duties of leadership across generations.
- Image prompt: Wide interior shot of a Benin palace wall covered with bronze plaques, a royal attendant pointing to one plaque while young apprentices observe, soft daylight from an open courtyard, historically appropriate palace setting

**Scene 5**
- Narration: Much was broken when British forces invaded Benin in 1897 and carried thousands of works away. The loss was not just beautiful objects, but a local system of memory, skill, and authority.
- Image prompt: Somber wide shot of Benin palace courtyard after the 1897 invasion, bronze plaques and carved objects being gathered into crates by foreign soldiers while Edo residents stand nearby, overcast light, historically respectful depiction, no modern logos

**Scene 6**
- Narration: Remembering Benin’s bronze tradition means respecting the makers, the descendants, and the calls for return. Ancient craft can still teach us that technology should preserve memory, not erase it.
- Image prompt: Close-up of contemporary Edo bronze caster hands shaping a wax model of a plaque in a traditional workshop, natural daylight, tools and clay molds on a wooden table, respectful documentary style, no on-image text
