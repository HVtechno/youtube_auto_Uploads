# Angkor’s Giant Reservoirs

At Angkor, the Khmer Empire built vast reservoirs and canals that turned a seasonal landscape into one of the ancient world's great urban regions. Their water system stored monsoon rain, supported rice farming, and reminds us how infrastructure can work with seasonal rhythms instead of against them. #AncientWisdom #Angkor #KhmerEmpire #WaterManagement #ForgottenHistory

**Scene 1**
- Narration: A thousand years ago, Angkor was not only a city of temples. It was a city of water, shaped by giant reservoirs called barays that captured the power of the monsoon.

**Scene 2**
- Narration: From about the 9th to 15th centuries, the Khmer Empire flourished in what is now Cambodia, where months of heavy rain could be followed by long dry seasons.
- Image prompt: Wide shot of a Khmer Empire landscape in ancient Cambodia during monsoon season, dark rain clouds over temple towers, flooded fields and canals, villagers in simple historical garments moving along raised paths, soft gray daylight

**Scene 3**
- Narration: Engineers dug canals, raised embankments, and built immense barays like the West Baray. Water was stored, redirected, and released through channels feeding fields, ponds, and temple neighborhoods.
- Image prompt: Medium-wide shot of Khmer laborers digging a canal with wooden tools and carrying earth in baskets beside a high embankment, a broad reservoir under construction in the background, late afternoon light, historically appropriate attire

**Scene 4**
- Narration: This was remarkable because it turned uncertainty into planning. Instead of treating floods and droughts as separate disasters, Angkor linked them in one system: store abundance, share it slowly.
- Image prompt: Split-depth wide shot showing water flowing from a stone-lined canal gate into green rice fields, Khmer farmers guiding water with small earth channels, temple silhouettes in the distance, clear morning light

**Scene 5**
- Narration: Over time, Angkor’s system faced stress from political change, maintenance demands, and possibly extreme climate swings. Modern cities often repeat the mistake: building fast, while forgetting who will maintain the water.
- Image prompt: Ground-level close-up of an overgrown ancient Khmer canal with cracked stone edges, tree roots and reeds blocking part of the channel, abandoned temple stones nearby, muted late-day lighting, no modern objects

**Scene 6**
- Narration: Remembering Angkor does not mean copying it stone for stone. It means respecting water as a living cycle, designing for seasons, and leaving future generations systems they can actually care for.
